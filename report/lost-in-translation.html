The same action.
A different word
in every app.

AI made translating Ethereum apps cheap and good. It did not make them agree. Each app localizes alone — so a Spanish, Portuguese, or Russian speaker meets a new word for the same button in every wallet they open.

"swap"→ Trocar Troca Fazer swap Efetuar swap Swap
The problem

Translation is solved.
Consistency isn't.

A modern model translates "Connect wallet" into flawless Spanish in a second. The catch: it does that independently for every app, with no shared reference — so the answers don't line up.

For a newcomer reading in their own language, that isn't cosmetic. When the same action is called three different things across the apps they use, the question stops being "how do I do this?" and becomes "am I even in the right place?"

When in doubt

The most common move is no move

For "wallet" in Spanish, the most popular choice across these apps isn't billetera or cartera — it's to keep the English word. Fluidkey ships "Conectar Wallet"; 1inch does the same.

Even the pros

A global exchange, split against itself

Binance has a paid localization team and four Spanish variants. Yet for "Convert" (its word for swap), Spain-Spanish says Convertir while Latin-American Spanish just keeps Convert. Same company, same language, opposite calls. The gap was never about budget.

Mostly cosmetic

Plenty of the differences are just synonyms. "Amount" shows up as three valid Spanish words — a reader understands all of them. Forcing one isn't worth much.

Sometimes wrong

But once you reach the jargon, it stops being a synonym game. Pick the wrong sense of a word in an app that moves money, and it's no longer cosmetic — it's a defect.

Aave · Russian — a lending app, where these words move your money
Repay→Вознаграждать→actually means "reward"
Collateral→Побочный→actually means "side / secondary"
Stake→Столб→actually means "pillar / post"
Withdraw→Отводить→actually means "divert away"
The same app's Spanish gets every one of these right — it's broken only in Russian, and nobody noticed. They read like a dictionary picking the wrong sense of each word. This isn't a style preference. A user is told "reward" when the button repays their loan.
CosmeticA synonym. The reader copes. Monto vs Cantidad.
ComprehensionEnglish left in — "slippage", "Stakear". The newcomer it's for can't read it.
CorrectnessThe wrong meaning. In an app that moves money, that's a hazard.
The proposal

One dictionary the whole
ecosystem can pull from.

A single open, machine-readable source of truth for how Ethereum terms are written in every language — that apps and the AI models translating them ground against. The wrong-meaning bugs get caught once, by a human, and every app pulls the same correct word.

01

AI drafts everything

Every term, every language, generated and published instantly. Translation was never the bottleneck.

02

Humans rule the hard calls

Native speakers decide the contested choices AI can't — translate "slippage" or keep it? coin a word that doesn't exist yet? — and put their name on it.

03

Everyone grounds against it

Apps pull it as a termbase; LLMs cite it via an open API. One steward decision fixes the word everywhere at once.

An honest example of why humans stay in the loop. The current draft glossary translates "slippage" to deslizamiento with high confidence — yet the live apps keep the English "Slippage". Which is right is a native-speaker judgment, not a model's guess. That call is the work.
